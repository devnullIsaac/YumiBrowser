# Frequently Asked Questions

## Why is this written in C instead of Rust?

Language preference, plus a specific interoperability goal: any part of Yumi Browser should be consumable by other applications regardless of their implementation language. C's ABI is the universal FFI target. Rust can bind to C APIs, but exposing Rust abstractions to C requires hand-written wrappers — most of Rust's interesting features (traits, generics, enums with data, lifetimes) have no C ABI representation.

Yumi Browser also deliberately builds on top of established open-source C/C++ components (OpenSSL, FFmpeg, FreeType, ICU, DuckDB, Dawn, and others) rather than replacing them with from-scratch rewrites.

## Why not libsodium?

Libsodium is excellent and would be the natural choice for a project that did not need post-quantum primitives. Yumi does, and libsodium's upstream has signaled that post-quantum support is not on the near-term roadmap. OpenSSL 3.x + oqs-provider is the practical path for applications that want post-quantum KEMs and signatures today, and it is where most projects with the same requirement have converged.

## Why Threefish and Skein for the symmetric layer?

Three specific reasons. **Future-proofing:** Threefish-1024 has a 1024-bit key, giving it substantial headroom beyond 256-bit ciphers under pessimistic assumptions about future cryptanalysis, including Grover-class quantum search. This headroom directly supports the long-term stability commitment — the stronger the primitive, the less likely a forced cryptographic migration. **GPU parallelism:** Threefish parallelizes cleanly on GPU hardware, and a working GPU path is part of Yumi's performance story for bulk operations. **Diversity at the primitive level:** the symmetric layer is independent of the AES/ChaCha family, so a hypothetical weakness in those ciphers does not affect Yumi's symmetric security.

But there is a deeper structural reason that is specific to Yumi's architecture. The **Group Registrar** operates on an assumption that the cryptographic primitives it uses will remain sound for the lifetime of the groups it governs. Every peer identity is derived from a Skein-1024 hash of an ML-DSA-87 public key. Every envelope encryption uses Threefish-1024-CTR + Skein-1024-MAC. Every audit log entry is signed with ML-DSA-87. If any of those primitives were to weaken to the point of practical attack, the registrar does not have a graceful degradation path — the peer IDs, the encrypted content, and the audit chain all depend on assumptions that cannot be swapped out without rebuilding the group from scratch.

This is why the margin matters so much. A cipher like AES-128 or ChaCha20 is well-suited to applications that can rotate keys, re-encrypt data, and migrate users to a new primitive on a routine basis. Under Grover's algorithm, a 128-bit symmetric cipher is reduced to an effective 64-bit post-quantum margin. That is not catastrophic for a web service that can force a password reset and re-derive keys overnight. It is catastrophic for a peer-to-peer group where the same symmetric key protects years of shared files, chat history, and governance decisions, and where migrating the group means re-inviting every member, re-establishing trust, and reconstructing the social graph.

The Group Registrar must change **very** infrequently — ideally never — because any cryptographic migration is a significant complication that can fracture the way Yumi Browser works in its ecosystem. This is why the project errs on the side of paranoia over complacency with known-standard cryptography. The goal is a strong probabilistic guarantee that the chosen algorithms will stand for the next 20 to 50 years, not merely that they are good enough for today's threat model.

This is also why Yumi Browser is intentionally designed with an **out-of-band invitation process** that moves away from public-key cryptography as fast as possible. The handshake uses the hybrid KEM (ML-KEM + FrodoKEM + BrainPool ECDH) only to establish initial contact and derive a symmetric transport key. Once that key is established, the bulk of the group's long-term security rests on symmetric cryptography, which is a more formidable adversary for anyone who wishes to breach it. The public-key layer is the front door; the symmetric layer is the vault. The front door is designed to be opened once and then left behind.

The AEAD composition is the standard Encrypt-then-MAC pattern (Bellare-Namprempre) with independent subkeys derived via Skein-MAC labels, length-prefixed AAD, and constant-time tag verification. It is an in-house composition in the sense that the specific gluing is Yumi's, but every piece of it is a standard pattern applied to analyzed primitives.

## What about memory safety?

MISRA-C compliance with Frama-C annotations to formally prove memory safety and absence of undefined behavior is a primary development objective, scoped to first-party Yumi Browser code. See [Current Development Focus](23-development-focus.md). Until that work is complete, the recommended deployment is Flatpak via Flathub (see [Recommended Deployment](18-recommended-deployment.md)), with a VM layer added for elevated threat models.

## Doesn't MISRA-C prohibit dynamic memory allocation?

No, this is a common misunderstanding. MISRA-C does not blanket-prohibit heap allocation. Section 3.1 of the MISRA-C handbook documents that guidelines, requirements, and advisories can be deviated from as long as extra care is taken and the deviation is documented. The relevant rules require that dynamic allocation is handled with specific documented care — allocation strategies, lifetime management, and failure handling. Yumi Browser's use of dynamic allocation is adherent to MISRA-C compliance via the standard's own deviation process, not a violation of it.

## How big is a typical group registrar?

Most private groups — under 100 people, which covers the vast majority of real-world uses — have a group registrar well under 5 MB. Joining a group that size is fast and cheap. The 200 MB cap is an upper bound for accommodating public content hubs with millions of peers, not a representative size. See Storage and Size Management.

## Do I have to use the Yumi GUI toolkit for my webapps?

No. The WebAssembly runtime exposes the underlying primitives (WebGPU, input events, text shaping) through documented host functions, and any toolkit that compiles to WebAssembly and consumes those primitives will work. You can bring your own GUI, port an existing engine, or write one from scratch — the platform is open at the API layer. See GUI Options for WebApp Developers for the alternatives and a sketch of the first-party toolkit's design goals.

## What happens if a cryptographic algorithm used by Yumi Browser is broken?

In the worst case, affected groups may need to be rebuilt on a version of Yumi Browser that uses replacement primitives. This is a structural consequence of peer-to-peer systems where identity, membership, and content are all cryptographically bound. The full discussion, including why Yumi uses very strong cryptography specifically to push this risk far out, is in the Long-Term Stability Commitment section, under "The Cryptographic Constraint."

But the question deserves a more direct answer here, because it is the single most important risk a user of Yumi Browser faces — and also the one Yumi's design is most deliberately built to prevent.

**First, some reassurance: the cryptography in Yumi Browser is far stronger than what most projects use today.** ChaCha20 and AES-256 are 256-bit ciphers. They are excellent, widely trusted, and have no known practical break. Threefish-1024 is a 1024-bit cipher. The security margin is not merely "larger" — it is exponentially larger. Under the standard Grover assumption, a quantum search halves the effective bit strength of a symmetric cipher, giving AES-256 and ChaCha20 an effective post-quantum margin of roughly 128 bits. That is a solid, respectable margin. Threefish-1024, under the same assumption, retains an effective margin of roughly 512 bits. The difference is not incremental; it is the difference between a lock with a 128-digit combination and a lock with a 512-digit combination. Both are secure. One has substantially more headroom against surprises.

This is not a criticism of ChaCha20 or AES. They are the right choices for applications that can rotate keys and migrate users on a routine basis. Yumi Browser is not one of those applications. The Group Registrar binds peer identities, encrypted content, and audit log integrity to specific primitives for the lifetime of the group. When the primitive is Threefish-1024, that lifetime can be measured in decades. When it is a 256-bit cipher, the same decades carry a smaller safety margin. Yumi chose the larger margin because the cost of being wrong — a forced migration — is high, and the cost of the larger margin — slightly more CPU work, already measured at 58–100 MB/s per peer — is negligible in practice.

**If the symmetric layer breaks — Threefish-1024 or Skein-1024 — the consequences are serious, but they are not the end of your data.** Every encrypted message, shared file, and audit log entry would become potentially recoverable by an adversary. There is no retroactive patch for broken cryptography. However, your content is not lost to *you*. The remediation is to create new groups on a version of Yumi Browser that uses different primitives, re-invite every member through out-of-band channels, and **migrate your historical content** — chat history, files, videos, pictures, and everything else — into the new groups. This is not a fun process. It is not an easy process. But it is a possible process. Your data survives; it just has to be carried across the boundary by hand. Think of it as moving house because the locks failed: the furniture comes with you, but you have to pack the boxes yourself.

**If the signature layer breaks — ML-DSA-87 — the consequences are different.** An adversary who can forge ML-DSA-87 signatures could impersonate peers and inject false governance actions. The integrity of the group registrar depends on signatures being unforgeable. A break here does not expose historical content (that is the symmetric layer's job), but it does mean the trust model for *future* actions in the group is compromised. The remediation is the same: new groups, new primitives, re-invitation, content migration. The old chat logs and files remain confidential; the old group's ability to make new trustworthy decisions is what ends.

**If a KEM breaks — ML-KEM-1024, FrodoKEM-1344, or BrainPool-P512r1 — the window of exposure is much smaller.** The KEMs are only used during session establishment and the initial handshake. An adversary who breaks a KEM cannot retroactively decrypt past sessions unless they recorded the handshake traffic and can now recover the session key. This is the "harvest now, decrypt later" threat that post-quantum cryptography is specifically designed to address. Yumi's triple-hybrid handshake means that all three KEMs would need to break simultaneously for a past session to be compromised, which is the point of the hybrid construction. If one KEM is discovered to be weak, the other two continue to protect past sessions. Future sessions on the affected primitive are at risk until the group migrates, but the historical bulk of the group's data — protected by the symmetric layer — remains untouched.

**Why this risk is unavoidable.** The alternative to binding identity, membership, and content to cryptography is to bind them to a centralized service that can rotate keys, re-issue credentials, and patch the trust model on the user's behalf. That is exactly what Yumi Browser refuses to do — the whole point of the project is that no centralized service sits between peers. The cost of that architectural choice is that cryptographic breaks cannot be papered over with a server-side update. The benefit is that there is no central server whose compromise, coercion, or shutdown would by itself affect group operation.

**Why Yumi's choices make this scenario genuinely unlikely.** A 1024-bit symmetric cipher has 512 bits of effective post-quantum margin under Grover. A 256-bit cipher has 128. Both are secure today. The difference is headroom — how much margin remains if the cryptanalytic landscape shifts in unexpected ways. Yumi is not betting that Grover is the last word on quantum attacks. It is betting that even if the future is more hostile than Grover alone suggests, 512 bits of margin is likely to survive longer than 128. Every extra bit of security margin is, in a very real sense, a year of group stability that does not require a migration.

The out-of-band invitation design compounds this effect. By moving from public-key to symmetric cryptography as quickly as possible after initial contact, Yumi minimizes the amount of long-term data that depends on the public-key primitives. The symmetric keys are what protect the group's history, and the symmetric keys are the ones with the oversized margin. The public-key layer is a handshake; the symmetric layer is the vault.

Users should understand this risk profile clearly before deploying Yumi Browser for long-lived groups. The risk is real, but it is also remote — pushed far into the future by design choices that prioritize margin over convenience. And if the remote scenario ever arrives, the path forward exists: new groups, new primitives, and the migration of everything that matters. It is work. It is not loss.

## Is LLM-generated code used in this project?

Yes, and this is disclosed up front because it's the kind of thing users deserve to know.

LLMs were used to scaffold parts of the codebase, including initial drafts of the UDP networking stack, the Group Registrar, and the GUI toolkit. Every line of LLM-generated code was reviewed by hand before shipping, with specific attention to memory safety, API consistency, and the failure modes that LLMs are known to exhibit — plausible-looking but incorrect edge cases, fabricated API calls that don't exist in the referenced library, and subtle off-by-ones. LLM-generated test cases are used to validate expected API behaviors, and those tests are themselves reviewed before being trusted.

Cryptographic code received the most scrutiny. No LLM output shipped in `src/crypto/` without being walked through against the original primitive specifications and against known-correct reference implementations. **The embedded Skein and Threefish code is not LLM-generated** — it is derived from the Schneier et al. reference implementation, as documented below.

One person reviewing their own LLM output is not the same as an independent audit, and this README does not claim otherwise. The architectural defenses are designed to contain implementation bugs — Wasmer isolates WebApps from the host regardless of bugs in Yumi's C code, and the Flatpak sandbox contains the blast radius at the OS level. These are layered mitigations, not substitutes for a real audit. The auditing guide on the roadmap is specifically designed to make external review possible, and external review is welcome.

## Where is the GUI toolkit? Can I use it for my own projects? Doesn't this violate the AGPL?

The GUI toolkit in `sdk/gui/` is not currently available as a standalone library. The author intends to commercialize it as a complete GUI toolkit suite for general-purpose use beyond WebAssembly and WebGPU — MISRA-C compliant, GPU-based, language-agnostic at the C ABI, and designed to the quality bar appropriate for mission-critical settings. This is disclosed here rather than buried because it is a possible perceived conflict of interest — a component of an otherwise AGPL project is being carved out for commercial sale — and users deserve to know.

The commercialization plan does not affect Yumi Browser's license, its source availability, or its feature set. The Yumi Browser host itself remains AGPL-3.0. What follows is the specific reasoning for why the commercial GUI toolkit can coexist with an AGPL browser host, since this is a legitimate question and deserves a real answer rather than a hand-wave.

**The WebAssembly / host boundary is an interface boundary, not a derivative-work boundary.** Yumi Browser operates under the same architectural principle as any web browser: the browser provides a runtime and exposes a set of APIs, and WebAssembly modules *request* those APIs at load time. The browser can grant the request, deny it, or revoke it later. The WebAssembly module does not link against the browser's source code, does not include the browser's source code, and does not modify the browser's source code. It calls across an interface boundary the same way a web page's JavaScript calls the DOM API in Chrome or Firefox — and nobody argues that shipping a closed-source JavaScript library means Chrome's license reaches into the library. Under this model, the GUI toolkit that runs inside Yumi Browser is not a derivative work of Yumi Browser. It is a consumer of Yumi Browser's public API, which is a very different relationship.

**AGPL-3.0's disclosure obligations attach to modifications of the licensed work itself.** AGPL-3.0 requires that modifications to the AGPL-licensed program be made available in source form to users who interact with the program over a network. The licensed program here is **Yumi Browser** — the C host, the networking stack, the crypto layer, the runtime bindings. Modifications to any of those are covered by AGPL and must be disclosed. WebAssembly modules running inside Yumi Browser do not modify Yumi Browser. They run *on top of* it, through the API boundary described above. The GPL family's long-standing distinction between linking/combining (which can trigger copyleft) and running separate programs that communicate through a defined interface (which does not) places the WebAssembly sandbox model firmly on the "separate program" side of that line: Wasmer isolates each module in its own memory space, each module only interacts with the host through explicitly exposed host functions, and the host can revoke that access at any time. This is the same relationship a database server has with its clients, or a window manager has with the applications it hosts — and neither of those relationships is considered to trigger copyleft across the interface.

If you fork Yumi Browser itself and modify its source, AGPL applies and you must disclose your changes. That obligation is unchanged. What AGPL does not do is reach across the API boundary into every WebAssembly module that happens to be loaded into a Yumi Browser instance.

**This is the same principle every web browser has operated under for decades — not a novel interpretation.** Some people have raised the question of whether running closed-source WebAssembly modules inside an AGPL-licensed browser host creates any kind of copyleft ambiguity. The author's position is that it does not, and this position is not novel: it is the same principle that Chromium, Firefox, and WebKit have all operated under for the last twenty-plus years. Those browsers host closed-source JavaScript and WebAssembly inside their runtimes constantly, and no one treats those modules as derivative works of the browser that hosted them. If the expansive reading were correct, the entire modern web would be in violation of its browsers' licenses, which is not how anyone reads those projects. Yumi Browser follows this long-understood principle rather than attempting to extend AGPL's reach into territory where it has never applied. The author does not pursue — and does not reserve the right to pursue — any enforcement theory that would require reading AGPL more expansively than every other browser project has for decades.

**The shipped default WebApps are the author's own copyrighted work.** Yumi Browser ships with a default set of WebApps (chat, file share, video streaming, picture gallery, animated emoji, music, livestreaming). These WebApps are authored and copyrighted by the same person who authors and copyrights Yumi Browser. Because the same copyright holder owns both, no license agreement between the two is required for them to ship together. Copyright law does not require a rights-holder to license their own work to themselves, and a copyright holder cannot violate their own license by choosing how to distribute their own work. This is a straightforward point but worth stating explicitly because it is sometimes misunderstood: enforcement authority over AGPL-3.0 belongs to the copyright holder of the licensed work, not to end users or third parties, and the author is the sole copyright holder of Yumi Browser's original code (the embedded Skein/Threefish reference implementation and third-party dependencies excepted, each of which is used under its own license).

**You can build your own GUI toolkit for Yumi Browser.** Because the boundary is an API boundary, anyone can write their own GUI toolkit targeting the Yumi Browser WebApp runtime. The API is documented in the `sdk/` headers shipped with the project, which are themselves part of the AGPL Yumi Browser distribution. You can implement a toolkit in C, Rust, Zig, or any language that compiles to WebAssembly, and it will work inside Yumi Browser on the same terms the first-party GUI toolkit does. You are also free to port an existing engine like Godot, Raylib, or Unity to target the Yumi Browser WebAssembly runtime, subject to those engines' own licenses. The commercial GUI toolkit suite is one option for WebApp developers, not the only one. The platform is deliberately open at the API layer so that alternatives can exist.

**Third-party dependency licenses are respected.** Every third-party dependency used by Yumi Browser (SDL3, Dawn, Wasmer, DuckDB, OpenSSL, FFmpeg, FreeType, HarfBuzz, ICU, oqs-provider, liboqs, libjuice, Clay, LibRaw, bzip2, libjpeg-turbo) is used under its own license terms, and the distribution respects those terms. No dependency license is circumvented by the architectural separation described above; the separation concerns the AGPL boundary of Yumi Browser itself, not the licenses of the components Yumi Browser builds on.

## Where does the Skein/Threefish implementation come from?

The Skein-1024 hash and Threefish-1024 block cipher implementation in `src/crypto/` is derived from [Skein3Fish](https://github.com/wernerd/Skein3Fish) by Werner Dittmann, which is itself based on the reference implementation by the Skein team (Niels Ferguson, Stefan Lucks, Bruce Schneier, Doug Whiting, Mihir Bellare, Tadayoshi Kohno, Jon Callas, and Jesse Walker). Threefish is the underlying block cipher of Skein — the Threefish code lives inside the Skein source files rather than in a separate `threefish.c`.

This is *not* LLM-generated code. It is an established reference implementation used directly.

## Why is the issue/discussion board not open to the public?

Open-source trolling is a well-documented phenomenon, and public issue boards on security-adjacent projects attract a disproportionate amount of it. The pattern is familiar: hostile demands, bad-faith nitpicking, exhausting arguments that are about the argument rather than the code, and the slow attrition of a maintainer's willingness to keep working on the project at all. A solo developer does not have the bandwidth to be both a productive engineer and a full-time moderator of that dynamic, and trying to do both produces neither.

Yumi Browser has its own Quality Assurance team that validates, improves, and fixes issues as they arise. Bug reports and feature discussions that come through that channel, or through sponsor access to the issue board, receive real engagement. Sponsors can vote on feature prioritization, propose new work, and participate in substantive technical discussion. Responsible conduct is expected in that space, which is most of the point of having it.

If you have something substantive to say about Yumi Browser and you are not a sponsor, posting about it publicly — on social media, on your blog, on a forum — is a perfectly reasonable channel. The author does pay attention to the public conversation about the project, and substantive criticism tends to surface one way or another. The closed issue board is not a silence-the-critics mechanism; it is a filter for the kind of engagement that moves the project forward versus the kind that drains the people working on it.

If you do not agree with how Yumi Browser development operates, forking under AGPL-3.0 is always available. We ask only that forks be renamed.

## Is it legal for me to use Yumi Browser where I live?

That depends on your jurisdiction, and it is not a question this project can answer for you. Yumi Browser is free and open-source software distributed under the AGPL-3.0 license and published as source code on public repositories. The cryptographic algorithms it implements are standardized or standards-track (ML-KEM, ML-DSA, FrodoKEM, BrainPool-P512r1) and are widely used in mainstream software, and the Skein / Threefish construction is a published reference design.

Some jurisdictions regulate the import, use, export, or redistribution of cryptographic software — for example through import licensing, sanctions programs, end-user restrictions, or general export-control regimes. **Compliance with the rules that apply where you live is your responsibility as the user.** The project and its maintainers are not in a position to tell you whether any particular use of Yumi Browser is allowed in any particular country. If you are unsure, check with someone familiar with the relevant rules in your jurisdiction before installing, using, or redistributing the software.

See also the Export Control Notice in [Cryptography](04-cryptography.md) and the project notices in [LEGAL.md](../LEGAL.md) and [README.md](../README.md).

## How does Yumi Browser engage with security researchers?

Security discussion is gated to vetted professionals, not because amateur input is unwelcome in principle, but because productive security review requires a shared baseline of technical context. The goal is to improve Yumi Browser's security posture, and the people best positioned to do that are people who can read the code, understand the constructions described in [Cryptography](04-cryptography.md) on their own terms, and bring findings rather than generic objections. An unbounded discussion channel with unvetted participants tends, in practice, to consume the mental energy of everyone involved without producing improvements to the software.

The design philosophy that drives this is the same one that drives the rest of the project: Yumi Browser does not place blind trust in any single recommendation, source, or authority. The hybrid KEM uses three independent public-key algorithms precisely so that no single one has to be trusted alone. Group joining uses out-of-band invitation as the primary channel so that no single communication medium has to be trusted alone. And the security review process filters for participants who can engage on the merits, so that the review itself does not become a single point of failure.

External cryptographic review through channels like NLnet NGI Zero, and the forthcoming auditing guide for independent reviewers, are the primary paths for substantive security input. Both are in [Current Development Focus](23-development-focus.md).
